Student loans are one of the most misunderstood financial tools in existence. You borrow money at 18, sign something, and don't think about it again until graduation.

Then the bills start.

Here's what you actually need to know about how student loans work, how interest compounds against you, and the strategies that make the biggest difference.

## How compound interest works against you

When you take out a student loan, interest starts accruing immediately on most loan types. On unsubsidized federal loans, interest builds while you're still in school — even though you don't have to make payments yet.

By the time you graduate, you may already owe more than you borrowed.

This is compound interest working against you: interest charges that themselves accrue interest over time.

## Federal vs private loans

Federal loans come with protections private loans don't offer — income-driven repayment plans, potential forgiveness programs, deferment and forbearance options. If you have both types, prioritize understanding your federal loans first.

Private loans are subject to whatever terms you agreed to with the lender. Read those terms carefully.

## The payoff strategies that work

**The Avalanche Method:** Pay minimums on all loans, then throw every extra dollar at the highest-interest loan first. Mathematically optimal — saves the most money over time.

**The Snowball Method:** Pay minimums on all loans, then attack the smallest balance first regardless of interest rate. Psychologically powerful — early wins keep you motivated.

Neither is wrong. The best strategy is the one you'll actually stick to.

## The one thing most people miss

Making an extra payment here and there feels small. But on a long repayment timeline, even an extra $50/month can shave years off your loan and save thousands in interest.

Check if your loan servicer applies extra payments to principal (they should by default, but it's worth confirming).

## Takeaway

Student loans aren't a life sentence, but they do require a strategy. Understand what you owe, know your options, and make a plan before the grace period ends.
