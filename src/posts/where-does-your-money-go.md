You've decided to start investing. That's huge. Most people never even get this far.

Then reality hits.

You open a brokerage app, hear people talking about 401(k)s, Roth IRAs, HSAs, and suddenly it feels like you need a finance degree just to make your first move. You don't want to mess it up. You don't want to put your money in the "wrong" place.

Here's the good news: there is no perfect first choice. There is, however, a smart order — and once you understand it, everything clicks.

## HSA (Health Savings Account)

If you qualify for an HSA, this is one of the most powerful places your money can go — and most beginners don't realize it.

An HSA is only available if you're enrolled in a high-deductible health plan (HDHP). Without an HDHP, you can't open or contribute to this account.

**An HSA has a triple tax advantage:**

- Contributions can reduce your taxable income
- Investment growth is tax-free
- Withdrawals are tax-free when used for qualified medical expenses

No other account combines all three.

## 401(k)

If you have a job and your employer offers a 401(k), this is usually the most natural starting point. The biggest advantage is the employer match — that's free money. Skipping the match is one of the most expensive mistakes new investors make.

**Most 401(k)s are traditional by default:**

- Contributions lower your taxable income today
- Investments grow tax-deferred
- Withdrawals in retirement are taxed as income

## Roth IRA

For many beginners, this is the most intuitive place to start investing. A Roth IRA is funded with after-tax money — you don't get a tax deduction today. Instead, you're buying future freedom. Your investments grow tax-free, and qualified withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free.

## A smart order for getting started

You don't need to use every account at once. A simple priority order works for most people:

1. Start with a 401(k) up to the employer match.
2. If you're eligible, contribute to an HSA.
3. Build long-term tax-free growth with a Roth IRA.
4. Add more to your 401(k) as income increases.

Even following the first two steps puts you ahead of the majority of investors.

## Final takeaway

When you're new to investing, the hardest part isn't choosing investments — it's choosing where to put your money. You don't need to make the perfect decision. You just need to make an informed one and start.

**Your first move doesn't need to be flawless. It just needs to exist.**
